Primary — Aour Glaz (Flax Blue)

An Aour Glaz — The Blue Gold

From the 16th to 18th centuries, the linen trade transformed Brittany into one of Europe's wealthiest regions. Flax seeds arrived from the Baltic at Roscoff, were cultivated in the Léon and Trégor, and the resulting toiles were exported worldwide via Morlaix, Landerneau, and Saint-Malo. The profits built the grand calvaires at Guimiliau, Pleyben, and Saint-Thégonnec — and the term "or bleu" (blue gold) referred to the delicate blue flowers of the flax plant that made it all possible.

Flax flowers range from pale sky blue to bright blue-violet, with deeper blue veins radiating from the centre. These are darkened versions suitable for brand use.

Secondary — Aour Balan (Broom Gold)

Aour Balan — Gold of the Broom

The genêt à balais (Cytisus scoparius) — called balan in Breton — carpets the landes and coastlands of Brittany each spring in brilliant gold. So central is this plant to Breton identity that it gave French the word "balai" (broom) and named towns across the region: Bannalec in Finistère, Ploubazlanec in Côtes-d'Armor. Flowering branches were hung in houses and stables as protection — the broom was both humble and sacred, a plant of sweeping clean and fertile new beginnings.

Genêt flowers range from pale lemon to deep golden-ochre. The base tone captures the warm, saturated gold of full bloom in late spring across the Breton moors.

Tertiary — Lin Violet (Flax Violet)

Lin Violet — The Other Blue of Flax

Botanical sources describe the flax flower (Linum usitatissimum) as "pale blue to bright blue-violet, with deeper blue veins radiating from the centre." Lin Violet captures that violet undertone — the shadow side of the flax petal, the colour you see when the light shifts. Where Aour Glaz is the open-sky blue of the flower, Lin Violet is its twilight cousin: more introspective, more sophisticated, equally rooted in the same plant.

The blue-violet range of flax petals. An alternative accent for contexts where the primary blue needs a softer or more contemplative companion.

Palette Rationale

  • Aour Glaz (#4A6FA5) — Named after the Breton term for "blue gold". A genuine connection to Brittany's flax trade history, not a marketing invention. The colour that built the calvaires.
  • Aour Balan (#D8A23A) — Named after the Breton word for genêt (broom), the golden flower that defines the Breton lande. The word "balan" gave French the word "balai" and named Bannalec, Ploubazlanec. Blue and gold together = lin et or, flax and broom.
  • Lin Violet (#5B6B9D) — The blue-violet undertone described in botanical sources for Linum usitatissimum. The twilight side of the same flax flower. Use as an alternative to Aour Glaz when a softer, more contemplative tone is needed.
  • All three colours trace back to two plants — flax (lin) and broom (balan/genêt) — both deeply rooted in Breton land and history.
  • The palette avoids generic tech-blue or startup-orange. Every colour has provenance.
  • Neutrals are named for the Breton coast: Ink, Slate, Storm, Mist, Cloud, Sand, Pearl.
